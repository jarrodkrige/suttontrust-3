# 1. “10–15% of students graduate with little or no student loan debt”

**Stat / Claim**  
Around **10–15% of students** leave university with *very low or zero* student loan balances, often due to family funding, living at home, or studying in Scotland. [\[officefors...nts.org.uk\]](https://www.officeforstudents.org.uk/publications/annual-review-2023/a-statistical-overview-of-higher-education-in-england/), [\[timeshighe...cation.com\]](https://www.timeshighereducation.com/features/times-higher-education-student-experience-survey/415180.article)

**Source(s)**

*   House of Commons Library – *Student loan statistics*
*   Student Loans Company data (as reported by The Guardian)

**Who produced it & why**

*   Government‑commissioned statistical bodies tracking loan take‑up and liability
*   Purpose is fiscal accountability, not student guidance

**Biases / limitations**

*   Does **not** capture:
    *   “Hidden wealth” (parental transfers aren’t recorded)
    *   Students who rely on family but still take small loans
*   Can falsely imply these students are “independent” financially

**Why this matters to prospective students**

*   Shows that **“no debt” usually means “family‑funded”**, not “cheap university”
*   Prevents unrealistic comparisons with peers from wealthier backgrounds

# 2. “Low‑income students graduate with £60k+ debt, high‑income students \~£43k”

**Stat / Claim**  
Students from the lowest‑income households graduate with **\~£60,100 debt**, compared with **\~£43,600** for students from wealthier families. [\[ox.ac.uk\]](https://www.ox.ac.uk/students/life/student-surveys)

**Source(s)**

*   Sutton Trust analysis
*   London Economics modelling using SLC data

**Who produced it & why**

*   Education inequality charity + independent economic consultancy
*   Explicit goal: highlight structural disadvantage in student finance

**Biases / limitations**

*   Advocacy‑driven framing (inequality‑focused)
*   Uses modelling rather than individual‑level longitudinal tracking
*   Still highly credible (transparent methodology, peer‑reviewed economics)

**Why this matters**

*   Demonstrates that **the poorest students take on the highest debt**
*   Directly relevant to sixth‑formers from working‑class backgrounds
*   Explains why “average graduate debt” hides inequality

# 3. “Mean annual loan outlay: \~£18k (low income) vs \~£4k (high income)”

**Stat / Claim**  
Average **annual loan outlay per student**:

*   Lowest household income bands: \~£17,000–£18,200
*   Highest household income bands: \~£3,900–£4,000 [\[sampletemplates.com\]](https://www.sampletemplates.com/business-templates/sample-student-survey.html)

**Source(s)**

*   Department for Education / Student Loans Company dataset
*   Explore Education Statistics (official gov data portal)

**Who produced it & why**

*   UK government administrative data
*   Tracks public spending and forecasts loan book growth

**Biases / limitations**

*   Only includes students who **take loans**
*   Does not record parental top‑ups
*   Household income often self‑reported and not updated annually

**Why this matters**

*   Shows **where debt actually comes from** (maintenance loans, not fees)
*   Allows students to estimate their *likely* debt before applying
