# Methodological Interrogation: Limits, Biases & Data Lag

## 1. Structural problem: education data always lags reality

### What’s going on

Most UK education outcome datasets:

*   Observe students **after** decisions are made
*   Report outcomes **15 months, 5 years, or even decades later**
*   Reflect labour markets that **may no longer exist**

Examples:

*   Graduate Outcomes survey = **15 months after course end** [\[greetingideas.com\]](https://greetingideas.com/missing-university-quotes-sayings/)
*   LEO earnings data = **5–10 years after study**, based on tax records
*   Apprenticeship evaluations often use **pre‑2023 cohorts**, before major reforms [\[news.liverpool.ac.uk\]](https://news.liverpool.ac.uk/2022/02/07/national-student-survey-tell-us-about-your-university-experience/)

### Why this matters now

Students starting university or apprenticeships in **2026** will enter:

*   A **post‑pandemic labour market**
*   An economy reshaped by **AI, automation, and remote work**
*   A higher education sector under **financial pressure and restructuring**

➡ **Conclusion:**  
Outcome stats describe **where people ended up**, not **where today’s students will end up**.

## 2. Graduate Outcomes data: valuable, but systematically optimistic

### Source

HESA Graduate Outcomes Survey (used heavily for degree vs apprenticeship comparisons) [\[greetingideas.com\]](https://greetingideas.com/missing-university-quotes-sayings/)

### Key biases

#### a) Response bias

*   Graduates in stable employment are **more likely to respond**
*   Disengaged or precariously employed graduates are under‑represented

HESA explicitly acknowledges weighting and non‑response issues [\[sampletemplates.com\]](https://www.sampletemplates.com/business-templates/sample-student-survey.html)

#### b) Timing bias (15‑month snapshot)

*   Favours **apprenticeships**, which convert directly into jobs
*   Penalises degrees where:
    *   internships occur first
    *   postgraduate study delays earnings
    *   career entry is non‑linear (e.g. creative arts, academia)

➡ This explains why apprentices appear dramatically better at 15 months — not because they *are always better long‑term*, but because of **design differences**.

## 3. Apprenticeship outcome stats: survivor and selection bias

### Source

Prospects / Luminate analyses of degree apprenticeships [\[surveysparrow.com\]](https://surveysparrow.com/templates/education/university-student-satisfaction-survey-template/)

### Key biases

#### a) Selection bias

*   Degree apprenticeships:
    *   are **highly competitive**
    *   often require strong grades **and** employer screening
*   Apprentices are **not a random sample** of students

➡ Outcomes partly reflect **who gets in**, not just the route itself.

#### b) Survivor bias

*   Data typically tracks:
    *   those who **complete** the apprenticeship
*   Drop‑outs or failed placements are weakly represented

➡ Results skew positive by excluding early leavers.
