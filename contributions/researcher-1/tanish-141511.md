# How Much Does Your Degree Really Matter Later — and What If You Change Your Mind?

## The short answer (before the scenarios)

*   **At the start of your career**, your degree choice and experience matter a lot.
*   **Later on**, your **experience and skills matter far more** than your subject or university name.
*   **Career pivots are normal** — most people don’t end up doing what their degree was “for”.
*   If you’re unsure, you should choose **flexibility + skills**, not a “perfect” course.

This isn’t motivational talk — it’s based on long‑term UK data tracking graduates 5–10+ years after university (LEO) and what employers actually look for when hiring. # Scenario 1: “I know what I want to do (or I think I do)”

**Example:**  
You want to be a doctor, engineer, solicitor, architect, teacher, or chartered professional.

### What the evidence says

*   In **regulated professions**, your **degree subject and accreditation matter for your entire career**.
*   Employer surveys and job‑posting data show degree requirements remain high in these fields. [\[advance-he.ac.uk\]](https://www.advance-he.ac.uk/knowledge-hub/student-academic-experience-survey-2025), [\[kcl.ac.uk\]](https://www.kcl.ac.uk/students/our-latest-student-survey-results-and-the-difference-your-feedback-makes-2024)

### How to approach university

*   ✅ Choose the **required subject and accredited course**
*   ✅ University choice can matter **early**, but accreditation matters **forever**
*   ✅ Get experience **inside** the profession early (placements, shadowing)

### Risk if you’re wrong

*   Pivoting is **possible but costly** (conversion courses, extra years, more debt)

💡 **Advice:** If you’re confident, commit — but be honest with yourself. If your confidence is based on pressure or prestige rather than exposure, pause.

# Scenario 2: “I have an idea, but I might change my mind”

**Example:**  
You’re thinking about law, psychology, economics, business, computing, sociology, or a joint honours degree — but you’re not 100% sure.

### What the evidence says

*   Longitudinal data shows **most graduates do not end up in a “degree‑required” job** long‑term, even if their degree helped them get started. [\[ox.ac.uk\]](https://www.ox.ac.uk/students/life/student-surveys)
*   Employer surveys consistently rank **skills + experience** above subject title after early career. [\[news.liverpool.ac.uk\]](https://news.liverpool.ac.uk/2022/02/07/national-student-survey-tell-us-about-your-university-experience/)

### How to approach university

This is the **sweet spot for flexibility**.

Focus on:

*   ✅ Courses with **transferable skills** (data, writing, analysis, teamwork)
*   ✅ Courses with **optional placements, internships, or applied projects**
*   ✅ Universities that **support work experience**, not just reputation

Add during uni:

*   Paid work or internships
*   Society leadership
*   Research projects
*   Skills portfolios (coding, writing, analysis)

💡 **Advice:**  
Your degree opens the *first door*. Your experience opens the next five.
