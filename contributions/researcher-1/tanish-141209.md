# 1. Sutton Trust internship & CV‑stacking statistics

*(e.g. “55% middle‑class vs 36% working‑class graduates do internships”, “40% rely on Bank of Mum and Dad”)*

### Primary sources used

*   Sutton Trust, *Unpaid and Underpaid Internships* (2025)
*   Guardian reporting based on the same dataset    [\[accommodat...udents.com\]](https://www.accommodationforstudents.com/student-blog/uks-10-universities-with-the-highest-dropout-rates), [\[timeshighe...cation.com\]](https://www.timeshighereducation.com/world-university-rankings/news/the-student-experience-survey-2014-uk-out-now), [\[slidetodoc.com\]](https://slidetodoc.com/student-satisfaction-uk-top-5-source-national-student/)

### Strengths

*   Large national graduate surveys (≈1,200+ respondents)
*   Repeated over time (2018 → 2025), allowing **trend analysis**
*   Cross‑checked with employer surveys

### Key biases & limits

**a) Self‑report bias**  
Graduates self‑report:

*   whether an internship was unpaid
*   whether parents supported them financially

➡ Likely **under‑reporting**, especially of informal parental help (“stayed with family”, “helped with rent”).

**b) Definition slippage**

*   “Internship” has no statutory definition
*   Informal shadowing, “work experience”, and private placements are inconsistently captured

➡ The **true advantage of well‑connected students is likely larger** than reported.

**c) Class categorisation bias**

*   “Working‑class” vs “middle‑class” is classified using parental occupation / education
*   This smooths over large variation inside each group

➡ Still directionally strong, but **not a precise individual predictor**.

### Time‑lag warning

*   Data mostly reflects cohorts graduating **2016–2023**
*   Internship pay regulation is changing (politically and legally)

➡ Reliably shows **structural patterns**, but **exact percentages may shift**.

✅ **Verdict:**  
High credibility for *inequality direction* and *mechanism*; medium confidence on exact percentages for 2026+ cohorts.

# 2. Recruitment outcome penalties (“same CV, different outcomes”)

### Primary source

*   UCL CEPEO / Early Careers Recruitment Data Hub (2022–2024) [\[ons.gov.uk\]](https://www.ons.gov.uk/surveys/informationforhouseholdsandindividuals/householdandindividualsurveys/studentexperiencesinsightsstudy)

### Strengths

*   Uses **employer administrative recruitment data**
*   Huge sample sizes (hundreds of thousands of applicants)
*   Controls for:
    *   university
    *   subject
    *   grades
    *   role
    *   employer
    *   region

This removes many confounders that plague surveys.

### Key biases & limits

**a) Sector bias**

*   Data drawn mainly from:
    *   law
    *   accountancy
    *   public sector
*   Less coverage of:
    *   creative industries
    *   SMEs
    *   self‑employment pathways

➡ Strong evidence for **elite professional pipelines**, weaker outside them.

**b) “Fit” remains unobservable**

*   Cultural capital, accent, confidence, interview polish are not measurable
*   Penalties observed likely **underestimate bias**, not overestimate it

### Time‑lag warning

*   Data ends in **2024**
*   AI‑screening, remote assessment centres, and contextual recruitment are evolving fast

✅ **Verdict:**  
Methodologically *very strong*; among the **best evidence available**. Patterns are structural and unlikely to disappear quickly.
